## THE REAL PROBLEM: COMPLEXITY WITHOUT SCALE

### Why Space is Expensive (And It's Not What You Think)

**The conventional wisdom blames propellant costs.** Wrong. Propellant is a low single-digit percentage of launch costs.

**The conventional wisdom says reusability is the answer.** Incomplete. Reusability isn't bad - but it's not the key problem either.

**The real issue: Excessive complexity + lack of scale = unaffordable access**

### The Scale Law

Modern complex systems become cheap through volume:
- **Automobiles:** Unfathomably complex, yet affordable. Tens of millions produced annually.
- **Smartphones:** Billions of transistors, costs hundreds of euros. Over a billion produced annually.
- **Rockets:** Comparable complexity, costs tens of millions. **Hundreds launched globally per year.**

**You can have complexity IF you have scale. You can have low scale IF you keep it simple. You cannot have both complexity AND low scale.**

Current rockets: maximum complexity, minimum scale. Fatal combination.

### Reusability: Helpful But Not Sufficient

Reusability reduces *per-launch* costs - but it doesn't solve the scale problem:
- Even with reusable boosters, global launch frequency remains in the low hundreds per year
- Reusability **adds complexity** (recovery systems, refurbishment, inspection)
- If reusability increases complexity more than it increases scale, it's a net negative

**Our approach:** Reuse the infrastructure (electromagnetic launcher), reuse the materials (tanks become orbital habitats), keep the flying hardware radically simple.

### Why the Space Elevator Fails This Test

The space elevator is often proposed as "fixed infrastructure." It fails because:
- **Throughput:** Single cable, climbers crawl at meters per second, extended time per payload
- **Insurance:** One cable break = total system loss, no redundancy
- **Cost/benefit:** Massive capital expenditure for throughput worse than existing launch systems

**We don't lack materials for a space elevator. We lack throughput to justify it.**

SNELS: Space elevator economics (amortized infrastructure) with **actual rocket throughput** (because we're still using rockets - just not for the first stage).

## TECHNICAL ARCHITECTURE

### System Overview

**Horizontal Access:**
- Extended tunnel from near Port of Motril through mountain
- TBM excavation through stable granite
- Rail transport of components from deepwater port

**Launch Cave:**
- Underground complex beneath Mulhacén
- Multiple fueling bays for parallel operations
- Projectiles positioned vertically for launch

**Vertical Launch System:**
- Combined vertical distance (underground shaft + tower on peak)
- Tower extends above Mulhacén summit
- Hybrid maglev: superconducting levitation + water-cooled copper acceleration coils
- Vacuum tube to eliminate atmospheric drag
- Moderate G-loading during acceleration phase

**Exit System:**

**Trap Door Seal - Passive Fail-Safe Design**
- Pressurized seal at top of vacuum tube
- Opens automatically from pressure differential as projectile approaches
- Compressed residual air rushes out first, creating buffer zone
- No active timing required - physics does the work
- Simple mechanical system, high reliability

Alternative approach (venturi assist with air-breathing engines) will be evaluated in Phase 1 demonstrator, but trap door represents baseline due to passive safety characteristics.

### Why Starting Velocity Matters More Than Peak Velocity

**The objection:** "Conventional first stages deliver much higher velocity"

**The reality:** Starting velocity at altitude beats higher velocity from sea level.

Conventional first stage delivers higher peak velocity but:
- Starts from zero at sea level in thick atmosphere
- Spends minutes fighting through dense air
- Can't use efficient vacuum-optimized engines until very high altitude

**SNELS delivers lower velocity but at altitude:**
- Start with booster velocity already achieved
- Out of thick atmosphere in under a minute
- Engines run near 1:1 thrust-to-weight initially (don't accelerate too fast in remaining thin atmosphere)
- Switch to full vacuum-optimized thrust in stratosphere **way earlier** than conventional rockets

**Result: Upper-stage-only configurations feasible (equivalently: single-stage-to-orbit from our exit conditions)**
- Double-digit dry mass fractions achievable
- Simpler vehicles, lower complexity
- True economy of scale

The question isn't "how fast at staging" - it's "how soon can you run vacuum engines at full thrust?"

### Launch Envelope: Infrastructure for Any Vehicle Design

**SNELS is infrastructure, not a vehicle program.**

The system accommodates any projectile design within physical parameters:
- **Diameter:** Large bore (expandable with larger tunnel diameter)
- **Mass:** Hundreds of tons gross launch mass
- **Propulsion:** Agnostic - hydrolox, methalox, hybrid, electric, or future systems
- **Configuration:** Customer-designed vehicles optimized for their missions

**Like a highway doesn't dictate truck design, SNELS doesn't dictate spacecraft design.**

Customers design their own vehicles to fit the launch envelope. The system provides:
- Electromagnetic acceleration to booster velocity
- Exit at altitude above weather layer
- Precise trajectory control
- High-cadence availability

### Launch Profile

1. Projectile transported horizontally through tunnel
2. Positioned vertically in launch cave and prepared
3. Electromagnetic acceleration: moderate G-loading over extended period
4. Trap door seal opens passively, buffering atmospheric transition
5. Projectile exits at supersonic velocity at altitude
6. Onboard propulsion systems engage in thin atmosphere
7. Vehicle proceeds to orbital insertion

### Critical Requirements Analysis

**Why coastal proximity is essential:**
- Feasible tunnel distance is limited
- Deepwater port required for component delivery (millions of tons of steel)
- Failed launch debris must fall over water, not populated areas

**Why eastward water corridor is non-negotiable:**
- Eastward launch gains Earth's rotational velocity
- Water corridor = zero population risk from launch failures
- Mediterranean offers extended unpopulated range

**Why geology matters:**
- Granite/stable rock: safe deep tunneling, structural support
- Volcanic geology: magma intrusion risk, seismic instability, unsafe for tunneling
- Sierra Nevada is stable granite batholith

**Why governance cannot be overlooked:**
- Multi-year project requires political stability
- EU legal framework protects orbital asset ownership
- Unstable regions risk expropriation, regulatory changes, armed conflict

**On altitude:**
- Taller mountains exist and would offer benefits from pure physics standpoint
- However, the combination of factors (port, corridor, geology, governance) outweighs raw altitude advantage
- Sierra Nevada's altitude is sufficient to exit above weather layer

**Conclusion:** Sierra Nevada is not "one option among many" - it is the **only site** that satisfies all critical requirements simultaneously.
